Batman Arkham Knight and the Scourge of Scale.
The "Arkham" series is a masterclass in superhero combat
and environmental storytelling.
But "Arkham Knight", the final entry,
struggled with a specific design hurdle: scale.
Specifically, the scale of Gotham City
and the inclusion of the Batmobile.
The Problem: Empty Spaces.
To accommodate the Batmobile’s speed,
Gotham had to be significantly larger than in previous games.
But a larger city means more "negative space"—
empty stretches between interesting locations.
This can dilute the density of the experience
that made the asylum and the city so engaging.
The Batmobile as a Crutch.
While the Batmobile is initially cool,
it quickly becomes a primary mechanic
that is forced into almost every mission.
Combat with tanks, puzzle-solving with the winch—
the car feels less like a tool
and more like a requirement.
It pulls the focus away from Batman’s core abilities:
stealth and hand-to-hand combat.
The Lesson: Bigger Isn't Always Better.
"Arkham Knight" shows that increasing the scale of a world
doesn't always lead to a better game.
If the mechanics aren't perfectly aligned
with the size of the environment,
it can lead to a bloated and repetitive experience.
Scale should serve the gameplay,
not the other way around.
Despite its flaws, Arkham Knight remains
a visually stunning and mechanically deep game.
But it serves as a cautionary tale
for any designer who thinks that "more"
automatically means "better".
Thanks for watching.
