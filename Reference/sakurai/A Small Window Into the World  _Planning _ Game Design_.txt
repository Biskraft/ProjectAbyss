In racing games and others, there's a pursuit of realistic visuals that look just like live-action footage. However, no matter how much you push graphics and simulation to the limit, it never truly feels like real driving. There are various reasons, but a major one is simply that the monitor is too small.
When looking at a monitor while sitting, your field of vision is only about this wide—around 30 degrees or so. This is obviously far inferior to the wide field of view one has when actually driving with their own eyes. In reality, a wider field of view is condensed onto the monitor, which creates a contradiction.
[Music]
TV monitors are very narrow. Therefore, you need to be very conscious of how much of that screen you are occupying. In racing games, a cockpit view should be the most realistic, but it feels like that perspective isn't chosen very often. This is likely because the area you can actually play in is drastically reduced relative to the screen size.
On the other hand, in VR, due to lower resolution, distant objects can sometimes appear blurry. This is problematic because you want to be able to see further. Also, in real driving, you can track things with your peripheral vision, but in VR, you have no choice but to turn your entire head in that direction.
Despite these drawbacks, I recommend trying a VR car game at least once. While there's a possibility of getting motion sickness, vehicles where the direction of travel is independent of the player's view are exceptionally compatible with VR.
Anyway, getting back to the point, thinking about how much screen space is used during gameplay is important. When playing an FPS, I often find it questionable that you have to aim at such tiny targets on such a large screen. Since you also need to view your surroundings, it's not necessarily a flaw, but it does lack the impact desired for entertainment. In Kid Icarus: Uprising, we compensated for this by making enemies several times larger than the player, though this depends on the game's direction.

The screen's width is like a narrow window. If you get too immersed in a game, you can lose sight of that.
