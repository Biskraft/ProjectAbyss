Smash Bros. is a game viewed from the side. Therefore, we place great importance on having a good silhouette from that perspective. Here are some common things that happen during development.
[Music]
First, as a premise, let's talk about the attack collisions (hitboxes) attached to various attack motions. Attack collisions don't directly follow the shape of a sword or fist; instead, they are designed to cover a wider area.
[Music]
Since Smash involves intense and rapid movement, it's a game with relatively large attack collisions. Without this, attacks would be difficult to land, resulting in a stressful experience.
Now, for a story from the motion production floor. There are times when an attack range doesn't cross the center line, making it hard to hit anything. Of course, I have them fix that.
Strictly speaking, regardless of where the motion passes during an attack, the planners can place the attack collisions to ensure hits. However, this makes the discrepancy between visual and collision even larger. While a certain level of difference in size between the visual and the hitbox is acceptable for the sake of fun, if even the position of the motion differs, the disconnect becomes too great.
Also, the timing of when the collision appears and the pose during a "clean hit" are important. At this point, "hitstop" occurs. It leaves an impression on the viewer, and if it's a finishing blow, the screen freezes momentarily. If the pose at that moment lacks a sense of impact, it's not good. So, for example, when swinging a sword, we might trigger the collision slightly ahead of the swing's visual position.
When creating attack motions, it's crucial to always be conscious of the collision's occurrence and its range. Technically, game balancing is handled by planners, so the motion team might feel it's outside their scope. However, since motion significantly impacts the game's "feel," it should be approached with the game itself in mind.
