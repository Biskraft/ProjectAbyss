the concept of interactive video is
something that's intrigued me for a long
time back in the '90s we experimented
with adding full motion video to games
which sometimes had endearing if
slightly comical results like the wing
Commander series or seventh guest and
other times Well less endearing
unfortunately at the time we could only
render lowquality video due to limited
storage capacity and many studios didn't
have the resources to professionally
produce highquality film work so the
dream of mixing media and using video
coupled with interactivity died as
developers realized that they could
produce better looking CG cutcenes than
anything they were going to capture on a
camera the torch then passed to the film
studios with the Advent of DVDs some
companies saw that adding interactive
elements to their movies was a decent
way to push DVD sales it was an xray you
could only get if you bought the home
release unfortunately the clunky
implementation and the lack of true
interactivity left us with products that
again just didn't live up to the dream
of interactive video which brings us to
today and Cloud chamber over many late
night cups of bubble tea James has
argued that we were approaching the use
of video fundamentally wrong if we
wanted it to be interactive he knew
interactive video couldn't just be
cutscenes because they don't have any
real interactivity but at the same time
he also knew that interactive video
can't be relegated to simple links
between alternate scenes that the player
chooses from at key points in the plot
of an otherwise non-interactive movie as
those destroy both the pacing of film
and the game's immersion unfortunately
He's just never had a very good answer
for what the proper method of using
interactive video might be and he'd
never really been forced to work on the
problem professionally so it remained a
topic of idle speculation for he and his
designer friends on nights when they had
run out of other things to talk about
until we saw a game called Cloud chamber
we covered Cloud chamber on an episode
of James recommends and it will almost
certainly come up on games you might not
have tried eventually but I want to talk
about it in depth here not because it's
perfect not by any means but because it
illuminates for us a form of
interactivity that is so integral to our
modern existence that it almost goes
overlooked a form of interactivity that
is perfect for interactive video titles
collaborative investigation Cloud
chamber is something between an ARG and
a mixed media project in it you the
player are tasked with unraveling the
events that went down at a research
institute in Denmark over the preceding
months but instead of having you take on
an avatar and walk around a virtual
landscape they've created a virtual
database of sounds and images and most
importantly film clips for you to sort
through with thousands of other players
the game has a cyberpunk vision of the
net where our online reality is
represented by different Landscapes and
all data is represented as nodes within
that landscape with the player hopping
from one node to the next in order to
piece together what's going on each node
contains some of the narrative for the
player to unravel be it a brief video
clip a text file or a picture but the
key is that these are often cryptic and
out of order the narrative itself is
handed to the player in a way that is
itself a puzzle and puzzles are
interactive if you've ever watched a
movie like primer or pie and sat around
with your friends afterward trying to
wrap your minds around what you just
been shown you know exactly what I'm
talking about that coffee shop moment
where you debate the meaning of a film
or try to piece together its story is
interactive it's an interactive element
that's already inherent to film by
putting puzzles and Mysteries into our
video we can create video that is
fundamentally interactive without having
to resort to cheap gimmicks or immersion
breaking menu choices Cloud chamber
helps to take that idea to the next
logical level not only by building its
videos entirely around this concept but
by creating a system within the game
that encourages players to discuss the
video and piece together the narrative
as a group there are real comment boards
built into the game and they basically
unlock new aspects of the narrative for
you once you've made enough comments on
the videos that other players find
worthwhile the system is far from
perfect but it forces the player away
from passively watching and instead gets
them to actively jot down notes try to
figure out where things go in the
timeline and unravel the Mysteries the
game provides in fact this is about the
only thing in the game that one could
consider a system or a mechanic in the
traditional sense but it does just
enough to push it from being a disjoint
movie to being a truly interactive use
of film Cloud chamber is only a starting
step in exploring this concept but it
serves as an excellent example of how we
can create interactivity in film without
relying on anything outside of what's
recorded from here I hope to see other
bold experiments that take up this idea
that interactivity can be inherent in
film itself and explore its use in more
traditional gaming it might turn out to
be a total failure but I would love to
see an Ace Attorney Style game play with
these Concepts and James has got some
thoughts on a killer horror game that
uses this as its premise a I don't think
these will ever be more than a niche but
I'm always interested in new elements
that we can bring into games and just by
changing how we think about video we
might be able to add another tool albeit
a very specific one to the designer's
tool belt I hope that gets you all
thinking I'll see you next week
