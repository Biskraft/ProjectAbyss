Once upon a time,
every game reviewer
would mention how long a game was.
Back then,
if your game didn't have at least 40 hours of content,
they were probably gonna dock you some points.
Even today games are often judged on their size.
I mean, how often have you seen
open world games advertise?
Just how big they are~~
But as my screaming to the void just demonstrated,
I think that's missing the point a little bit.
The better question might be to ask:
Is all of that content actually engaging?
Which brings us to today's topic:
Designing games
to respect the player's time.
Listing hours of content
or world size,
are just ways to convince you
that a game has enough content to justify its cost.
And this is important.
Because all of us
would rather pay 60 dollars to get 40 amazing hours of content
rather than just four.
But "Is that world amazing?",
that's really the important part.
Because along the way,
we should also be asking:
"How much of a game's content
is actually worthwhile?"
An open world
might have a hundred kilometers of space,
but is it actually enjoyable to traverse,
or is it just filled with repetitive busy work?
Because, let's be real.
Eight hours
of amazing content
is way better than 80 hours of an okay slog.
Games should respect your time.
Plus,
with the tons of recreational media 
we all engage with these days,
not to mention, you know.
Actual life stuff,
such as family obligations,
doing errands,
hanging out with your friends,
or learning a skill.
Developers should understand:
that your time
is actually as much of an investment as your money.
You can probably remember parts of games
that just seemed designed
to waste your time.
You know, those fetch quests 
where the mayor asks you to find some trinket,
but when you bring it back to him,
he then tells you to take it 
to the blacksmith down the street
that you just walked by.
Couldn't he have just told you 
to deliver it to that guy at the start?
Yeah.
Yeah, he could.
Then,
there's the UI side.
A lot of games have obtuse UI,
where doing common tasks
still require multiple button presses
when they really shouldn't.
I mean, think about the number of times
you've had to dive several menus deep 
for something in a game,
you found yourself doing all the time.
And that's why
when we're testing our UI,
it shouldn't just be for useability,
but also
for priority.
To see if it's streamlined
for the tasks the players are doing the most.
And then there's the uncategorized stuff.
Like unskippable splash screens 
every time you turn on the game,
or games that re-download 
their entire client every time they patch.
All these things
add up over time.
Of course, that doesn't mean
that a game can't be slow paced.
I mean some of the greatest games have a slow build
or feel like a relaxing summer afternoon.
It just means
that even in slow paced games,
the game
shouldn't be wasting your time.
Which leads us to the next point:
Pacing.
Okay, show hands.
When talking about a game,
has a friend ever said to you:
"Oh, you just have to give it a chance,
it gets amazing~ a hundred hours in.
(laughing)
Oh, sweet child.
If I have to put a hundred hours,
or 40 hours, or heck,
10 hours
into a game,
in order to quote
"get to the good part" unquote.
Something's gone wrong.
If your players have to prove themselves worthy 
to experience your real content,
Then that simply isn't respecting your player's time.
Putting an arbitrary grind
into your game,
just to keep players playing your game longer,
just does a disservice to everyone.
Because while the grind is good for business in the sense that it means
people are engaging with your game longer,
it's actually
terrible for business,
because plenty of people will simply bounce off
and put your game down.
Because they were gated from the good stuff
by that initial grind.
That's not to say that having some grinding elements in your game
is always bad.
It's 100% true,
especially when building MMOs
that you need to have
something that keeps your players from burning through all of the content
and quitting
before you finish building the new things
for them to do.
But even in that case,
you should be asking yourself:
"Is the activity
the player is grinding
inherently engaging?"
If it's something that the players would do 
without the skinner box reward,
if it's something that they would be doing
because it's something they were coming to your game for anyway,
then it's totally fine to attach a grind to.
It's just not okay
to make your players feel like they're investing hours
into the grindy bits of your game,
simply to get to something else.
So,
how does a game respect a player's time?
Simple.
Streamline any aspect of your game
that doesn't feel
like playing the game.
Modern turn-based RPGs of all things
are a great place to look.
The genre originally gained a reputation over the years
of being grindy and boring
by design.
So, a lot of recent games in the genre
are finding ways to cut a lot of the redundant stuff.
A great example of this,
is "Poke'mon Sword and Shield".
Breeding and hatching eggs takes less time,
you no longer have to trek back to a PC 
each time you want to switch Poke'mon.
And fishing no longer has a chance of nothing biting.
Man, I know my dad wishes real life would get that patch.
None of these examples diminish the gameplay,
they just made those specific actions 
less time consuming.
Other RPGs also include features like speeding up random encounters,
or having quests automatically complete
without returning to the quest giver.
Doing this
allows the player to focus on the core experience they're coming to the game for,
and makes them feel like the game was worth their money,
far more than any extra hours spent on these tasks ever would.
So, when designing your game,
or even picking up your next one to buy,
It's worth stepping back and asking:
"Does this game respect my time?"
"Is it actually interesting or fun to clear out all those enemy strongholds
or is it just something else to
do?"
How much of the game is just trivial busy work,
how much of it doesn't challenge the player,
or enhance their experience
in any way?
As a designer,
if you can identify those moments 
and rework or eliminate them,
then players will be able to get right into
what's engaging about your game,
leaving them with positives,
rather than thinking 
about the negatives.
And as a player,
looking at these aspects of a potential purchase,
will help you vote with your dollars on the experiences you want to spend your time on.
Because at the end of the day,
games should be about more than just killing your time,
every hour you invest in a game
could be spent on learning something new,
or catching up with friends,
or watching that show you keep meaning to,
or even playing other games on your Steam list.
So, therefore,
the games you end up playing
really should respect your time.
