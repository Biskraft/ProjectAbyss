Say, are you looking for a great way to 
design a character for a book, D&D session,  
game you're designing, or heck, really anything 
narrative? Well, do I got a method for you,  
because recently, rather than starting with 
an archetype or alignment, James has been  
playing around with building his characters 
around different real-world philosophies,  
and the results have been phenomenal and 
you know sometimes hilarious. Of course,  
by real-world philosophies, we 
mean just that. For instance,  
a deontological character would have a strict 
code they live by and won't deviate from, whereas  
a utilitarian character would always be looking 
for the greatest good or least harm, regardless  
of cost. But what makes this so interesting is 
that these systems always break down. I mean,  
if we'd found the perfect moral structure, that 
would be it, and we'd have you know solved ethics,  
but spoiler alert, we haven't! So, no matter what 
ethical philosophy you choose for your character,  
it's going to end up running straight into 
a brick wall. Oh, does your deontological  
character say violence is always wrong? Well, 
does that mean they don't lift a finger when  
a whole village is slaughtered in front of 
their eyes? Would your utilitarian character  
consider framing an innocent man for murder to 
prevent riots, say against a minority group?  
It's incredibly difficult moments like these that 
create vibrant opportunities for character growth  
and to explore real, meaningful moral dilemmas. 
So the next time you're creating a character,  
why not open up the philosophy section of 
Wikipedia and see where that rabbit hole takes ya?
