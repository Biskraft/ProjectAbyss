When making a rulebook for a tabletop
game, James says there are basically
three things you're always wanting to
deliver on: clarity, brevity, and
narrative. But all of those things are,
you guessed it, at odds with each other.
To achieve maximum clarity, you have to
provide specificity, giving edge cases,
and defining unique interactions. But by
definition, that's not going to be as
brief as possible, and narrative is
always going to be mushy. Either it's
totally separate from the rules, in
which case it interrupts people's train
of thought, or it's part of the rules,
so you're often sacrificing some of the
crunchy rigor of the rules to deliver
them in a narratively meaningful way.
And of course, narrative isn't the most
effective way to deliver your rules.
You're going to punctuate things with
cool action sequences or clever turns of
phrase that pad your word count. So,
when building your rule book for your
game, you have to decide which of these
elements is most important to the
experience you want to deliver to your
players. because each word you use is a
trade-off. And if you don't get the
balance just right for what you're going
for, your players are going to quit
before they even learn the game.
