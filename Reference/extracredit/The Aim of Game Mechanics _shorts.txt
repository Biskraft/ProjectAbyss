When we do something in real life or in
a game, that action has meaning. Raising
a gun to your shoulder, telling a field,
spending time with one person instead of
another. All of these things contain a
story within them. But it runs even
deeper. Like the slowness and cycle of
loss in a Souls game also sets the tone
for that world. It itself says something
about the adventure you're partaking in.
And this is something that James has
been thinking about a lot since he's
been working on the Cyberpunk card game.
Oh yeah, in case you missed the
announcement, that's where he's been
working on his dream project. I have
personally played it and it is rad as
hell. But back to the topic at hand. In
games, we spend so much time inactive
play that we can't afford to simply
ignore what our mechanics are saying if
we want players to really feel immersed
in our tale. For instance, in James'
cyberpunk card game, there's a
cooperative poker mechanic that
represents uncertainty, the living on
the edge nature of Night City, and the
fact that without people around you
giving you a hand, you're never going to
make it very far. Even Johnny Silverhand
knew that. So, as you're looking at the
games you're playing or building your
own, be sure to ask yourself, what are
the mechanics saying? Do they fit with
the story the rest of the game is trying
to tell? And if you like cyberpunk,
